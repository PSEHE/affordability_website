# Home Energy Financial Burdens and Solutions

Energy costs impact every household, but the burden is not shared equally, and solutions to reduce costs have a wide range of efficacy. Better data is needed to inform strategic suites of interventions.

01 • Costs for Energy at Home

### What households pay to run their homes

In 2024, American households collectively spent over XXX billion dollars to power, heat, and cool their homes.

- **Total spending:** approximately $285 billion for electricity and heating fuels
- **Low-income households** (earning less than 80% of area median income) paid $120 billion to meet their energy needs
- This represents **42%** of total residential energy spending

02 • Household Budget Impacts

### When energy costs are too high

**Two-thirds** of low-income households spend more than 6% of their gross income on energy (ACEEE, 2020). As a result, energy costs create significant burdens for low-income families.

03 • Residential Greenhouse Gas Emissions

### Home energy and climate impacts

Residential energy use is a major contributor to U.S. greenhouse gas emissions. Strategic interventions can reduce both costs and carbon.

- **Total emissions:** 303 million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent from home energy consumption in 2024 (US Energy Information Administration) representing **6%** of direct U.S. emissions
- Energy use from low- to moderate-income households contributes **40%** of home energy emissions
- Major opportunities exist to cut emissions while alleviating high bill burdens

04 • Why This Matters

### Affordability and equity go together

Energy affordability is both a social equity and climate issue. Better data helps inform strategies and direct resources where they have the greatest increase in prosperity.

- **Health & Safety:** Households struggling with bills face increased risks from inadequate heating, cooling, and unsafe energy practices (like using the oven for heating)
- **Upfront Costs:** Low-income families cannot afford efficiency upgrades or electrification that would reduce bills and emissions
- **Program Gaps:** Weatherization, efficiency, and assistance programs exist but lack detailed local data to target investments effectively
- **Energy Equity:** Equitable solutions require understanding energy burdens across different demographics and geographies
