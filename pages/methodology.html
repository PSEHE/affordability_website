Overview

The National Energy Affordability Tool estimates household-level energy costs, consumption, and emissions for all occupied housing units across the United States — down to the census tract level. Rather than relying on direct measurements (which don't exist at this scale), we build a synthetic population: a statistically representative set of simulated households in every tract, each with realistic characteristics, energy use, and costs.

The pipeline draws on several large national datasets — household surveys, utility records, and census data — and chains them together through five modeling steps. The result is a tract-level dataset covering all ~84,000 census tracts in the contiguous U.S., suitable for identifying energy burden hotspots, demographic disparities, and policy intervention opportunities.

Full technical documentation is being prepared for peer-reviewed publication. This page provides an accessible summary of the methods for practitioners and policymakers working with the tool.

Modeling Pipeline

The pipeline moves from census geography down to individual household estimates in five steps. Each step feeds into the next: the allocated households receive estimated housing characteristics, then energy consumption, then costs, then emissions.

1

Household Allocation

ACS PUMS

Each census tract in the U.S. has known demographic and housing characteristics from the American Community Survey (ACS) — things like how many renter households there are, the income distribution, the share of multi-family homes, and the predominant heating fuel. But the ACS reports these as aggregate counts, not individual households.

To get individual households, we draw from the Public Use Microdata Sample (PUMS), which contains detailed records for real (anonymized) households. Using integer programming optimization, we select a combination of PUMS households for each tract whose collective profile matches the ACS counts across 70 demographic and structural variables simultaneously — income brackets, housing type, heating fuel, year built, household size, and more.

The optimization minimizes geographic distance between the source PUMS geography and the target tract, so allocated households are drawn from nearby areas with similar regional characteristics.

Why not simpler methods? Standard reweighting approaches like iterative proportional fitting can only enforce two-way constraints and produce fractional households. Integer programming enforces multi-way constraints and produces whole households — which matters for accurate energy modeling.

2

Housing Characteristics Estimation

AHS

The allocated PUMS households carry many characteristics directly, but a few attributes critical for energy modeling — primarily floor area and air conditioning type — are not reliably observed in PUMS. These are estimated using machine learning models trained on the American Housing Survey (AHS).

For floor area, a Gradient Boosting model uses housing structure (rooms, building type, year built, tenure), household demographics, and geographic division as predictors. For AC type — whether a household has central air, a window unit, or none — a similar approach is used, with metro-specific models trained where local AHS data is available (35 metros), since AC patterns vary dramatically by region.

3

Energy Consumption Estimation

RECS 2020 NOAA

Energy use is predicted separately for up to 14 end-use categories — space heating by fuel (electricity, natural gas, propane, fuel oil, wood), space cooling, water heating, appliances, cooking, and lighting/other. Models are trained on the Residential Energy Consumption Survey (RECS) 2020, which provides detailed energy use data for ~18,500 U.S. households.

The appropriate model type is chosen for each end-use based on the statistical properties of that energy stream — whether consumption is near-universal or zero-inflated, and how skewed the distribution is. Most end-uses use Gradient Boosting regression; a few use linear models or Random Forest where those perform better on out-of-sample data. Climate inputs (heating and cooling degree days from NOAA) are included to capture geographic variation in demand.

End-use predictions are summed to total household energy consumption by fuel type.

4

Cost & Burden Calculation

EIA Form 861 SEDS

Predicted energy consumption (in BTU or kWh) is converted to annual energy costs using local utility rates and regional fuel prices. Electricity rates come from EIA Form 861, which maps utilities to their service territories, allowing us to apply the correct rate for each household's location. Natural gas, propane, fuel oil, and wood prices are drawn from the State Energy Data System (SEDS).

Energy burden — the share of household income spent on energy — is calculated by dividing annual energy cost by annual household income from the PUMS record. Households spending more than 6% of income on energy are commonly considered energy-burdened; those above 10% are considered highly burdened.

For households where utilities are included in rent (approximately 2–4% of households), payment status flags from PUMS are carried through the pipeline so these households can be distinguished in analysis.

5

Emissions Estimation

eGRID

Greenhouse gas emissions are estimated for each household's energy consumption. Electricity emissions use subregional emission factors from EPA's eGRID database, which captures variation in grid carbon intensity across the country. On-site fuel combustion (gas, propane, oil, wood) uses standard combustion emission factors.

Emission estimates are provided in CO₂-equivalent, allowing comparison across fuels and informing analysis of how fuel-switching or efficiency improvements would affect both costs and emissions.

Primary Data Sources

The pipeline integrates six major national datasets. Each plays a distinct role in the modeling chain.

🏘️

American Community Survey

ACS 5-Year · U.S. Census Bureau

Tract-level counts of households by income bracket, housing type, tenure, heating fuel, year built, household size, and race/ethnicity. These serve as the hard constraints for household allocation.

👥

Public Use Microdata Sample

PUMS · U.S. Census Bureau

Anonymized individual household records with detailed demographics, housing characteristics, income, and housing costs. The source pool from which households are allocated to tracts.

⚡

Residential Energy Consumption Survey

RECS 2020 · U.S. EIA

Nationally representative survey of ~18,500 U.S. households with detailed end-use energy consumption. Used to train all energy prediction models.

🏠

American Housing Survey

AHS · U.S. Census Bureau

Detailed housing unit characteristics including floor area, AC type, and utility payment status. Used to train housing characteristics models, with metro-level samples for 35 major metros.

🔌

EIA Form 861 & SEDS

U.S. Energy Information Administration

Utility service territory boundaries and electricity retail rates (Form 861), plus state-level residential fuel prices by type (State Energy Data System). Used to translate consumption to costs.

🌿

eGRID & NOAA

EPA · NOAA

EPA's eGRID subregional electricity emission factors for CO₂ intensity calculations. NOAA heating and cooling degree days as climate inputs to energy models.

Model Selection

Rather than using a single model type for all energy end-uses, we assign each end-use to a model family based on the statistical properties of that energy stream — observable before any model is trained. This approach avoids the appearance of empirical cherry-picking while still allowing the most appropriate method for each case.

Energy consumption models

Four model types were evaluated through 10-fold cross-validation on RECS 2020 data: Gradient Boosting regression, Random Forest, linear regression, and log-linear regression. Performance was compared using a cost-weighted error metric that translates BTU prediction errors into dollar errors — which weights electricity errors more heavily than gas errors, reflecting electricity's higher price per BTU.

End-use type Example end-uses Model used Reason
High-prevalence continuous Space cooling, space heating (electric) Gradient Boosting Best performance on high-priority end-uses; handles non-linearity well
High-prevalence, small sample Heat pump heating Random Forest Bagging reduces variance when fewer training examples are available
Moderate right-skewed Appliances (electric), cooking Linear or Log-linear Simpler models outperform on moderately skewed distributions
Zero-inflated (few users) Gas appliances, propane, fuel oil Linear Tree models overfit the zero mass; linear model most calibrated

Across all end-uses, Gradient Boosting reduces the cost-weighted prediction error by approximately $14 per household per year compared to the Random Forest baseline used in earlier versions of the pipeline.

Why income is not a predictor

A natural question is whether household income should be used to predict energy consumption. Our analysis found that income adds negligible predictive power (less than $2/year improvement in cost-weighted error) once building characteristics are already included. This is because income's effect on energy use operates largely through housing — lower-income households tend to live in smaller, older homes, and these characteristics are already in the model. Using income directly would introduce potential bias and conflate energy consumption with ability to pay.

How the Decision Support Tool Works

The underlying dataset represents individual simulated households at the census tract level — far too granular for direct use in an interactive web tool. The decision support tool works with a processed, aggregated version of this data and adds a scenario modeling layer that lets users explore how energy policy changes would affect costs and burdens.
